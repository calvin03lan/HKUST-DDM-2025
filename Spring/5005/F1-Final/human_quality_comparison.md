In my view, the two outputs still do not differ in any fundamental way. Their main conclusions are broadly aligned, which suggests that the original telemetry already contains enough signal to support baseline single-lap judgments. What changes after feature engineering is not the truth of the result, but the shape of the reasoning process. The structured version gives the model a clearer route: which variables deserve priority, which comparisons are meaningful, and how to connect observations into a coherent argument. That makes the analysis feel less noisy, less repetitive, and easier to control from a human perspective.

At the same time, I think both models reveal a shared weakness: they tend to drift beyond the scope of a single lap and imply multi-lap interpretations without fully justifying that leap. For example, both can move toward tire degradation, strategic implications, or broader performance trends, but neither sufficiently confronts the core methodological limitation: one lap cannot robustly support claims about longitudinal behavior. Without lap-to-lap variation, stint context, fuel evolution, traffic effects, and environmental changes, those higher-level inferences are at best hypotheses, not conclusions.

This is exactly why feature engineering should be paired with boundary engineering. It is useful to guide the model toward richer metrics, but equally important to guide it away from over-claiming. A stronger workflow is not just “better features in,” but also explicit guardrails on what the data can and cannot prove. From that perspective, the real value of feature engineering is threefold: it improves reasoning efficiency, increases interpretive focus, and—when designed responsibly—helps enforce epistemic discipline.
