# Output Quality Comparison Between `pia_jp_26_raw.ipynb` and `pia_jp_26_str.ipynb`

## Executive Summary

The two notebooks were produced with the same prompt set and the same large language model, so the most important difference between them is the form of input data. `pia_jp_26_raw.ipynb` works directly from the downloaded raw telemetry CSV and lap summary JSON, while `pia_jp_26_str.ipynb` works from processed structured data, including a corner-enriched telemetry file and corner metadata. Overall, the structured-data notebook produces the higher-quality final analysis report. The raw-data notebook remains valuable because it is more transparent about how metrics are reconstructed from original telemetry, but it is weaker in narrative coherence, corner-level interpretation, modeling discipline, and reader-facing presentation.

The two reports do not disagree on the core facts. Both identify the same basic lap context: 707 telemetry samples, a lap time of 92.996 seconds, a peak speed of 317.0 km/h, HARD tyres, and a constant TyreLife value of 31. This means the difference is not caused by one notebook failing to load the data correctly. Instead, the difference appears when the notebooks move from basic telemetry description into higher-level judgment: where lap time was shaped, which driving phases mattered, and which conclusions are actually supported by the available data. At that level, the structured-data version is more convincing.

## Data Validation and Reproducibility

The main strength of the raw notebook is that it rebuilds its analytical pipeline from the original files. It parses time, distance, speed, throttle, brake state, gear, coordinates, curvature, and sector boundaries from the raw CSV and JSON. It also performs useful quality checks: the lap end time matches the summary lap time with 0.0 ms error, the sector sum matches the full lap time with 0.0 ms error, distance is monotonic, and the recorded speed range is 73.0 to 317.0 km/h. These checks are meaningful because they show that the sector distances, braking zones, and speed traps are computed inside the notebook rather than merely described from pre-existing summary fields.

The structured notebook handles validation in a more report-ready way. It reports the same row count, lap time, approximate 5.72 km lap distance, and speed range, but it also adds sampling diagnostics: a median sampling interval of 0.139 seconds and a maximum interval of 1.039 seconds. That detail improves the quality of the analysis because it explains why distance-binned and windowed calculations are more reliable than point-to-point derivatives. The structured notebook also reports zero original missing values and explicitly states that it uses `structured_data_with_corners.csv` as the analysis frame. This makes the analytical dependency clear: the report is not only using cleaned telemetry, but also structured semantic labels generated during preprocessing.

However, the structured notebook has an important dependency cost. Its corner-level conclusions rely on `corner_info.csv` and `structured_data_with_corners.csv`. If the corner windows or corner labels are wrong, the downstream corner conclusions inherit that error. The raw notebook is less semantically rich, but it stays closer to the original evidence chain. Therefore, the raw notebook is better for auditing the base calculations, while the structured notebook is better for presenting a mature technical report.

## Metric Design and Interpretive Depth

The raw notebook covers four major analysis areas: sector performance, speed traps, braking zones, and a tyre stress proxy. This is a solid technical structure. It identifies Sector 2 as the longest and most decisive part of the lap, taking 41.221 seconds over roughly 2524 meters. It identifies the strongest speed-trap window around 4041-4862 meters, reaching 317.0 km/h. It also identifies the heaviest braking event around 5214 meters, with a speed loss of 173.9 km/h and a peak deceleration of -48.61 m/s². These outputs are useful and technically grounded, especially for demonstrating that raw telemetry can be transformed into meaningful performance indicators.

The structured notebook goes further. In addition to sectors, speed traps, and braking behavior, it calculates full-throttle distance share at 63.2%, brake distance share at 17.3%, coast distance share at 4.1%, gear usage, speed distribution percentiles, corner entry speed, apex minimum speed, exit speed, speed loss, brake distance, brake share, and throttle reapplication distance. The corner analysis is the clearest quality improvement. It can state that Corner 11 has the largest entry-to-minimum speed loss, 103.3 km/h, and is also the slowest point of the lap at 73.0 km/h. It also identifies Corner 9 as having the latest 80% throttle reapplication, 35.5 meters after the apex. These conclusions are more useful than abstract braking-zone labels because they connect performance loss to track geometry and driver phase.

This makes the structured notebook more interpretively rich. It does not simply say where braking was heavy; it explains which corner the braking belongs to, how much speed was lost from entry to apex, how much recovery occurred on exit, and whether throttle commitment was delayed. That moves the report from telemetry description toward racing-performance interpretation.

## Modeling Boundaries and Risk Control

The tyre degradation section is one of the most important quality differences. The raw notebook creates a single-lap tyre stress proxy and reports first-half versus second-half traction acceleration, peak stress index, and cumulative stress. It clearly says this is not a true multi-lap degradation model, but the presence of numerical stress and grip proxy values still creates a risk: readers may interpret the section as evidence of actual tyre degradation. With only one lap and a constant TyreLife value, differences between the first half and the second half of the lap are likely influenced by circuit layout, corner sequence, and speed profile rather than tyre wear.

The structured notebook is more disciplined. It includes a dedicated section titled “Tire Degradation Modeling: Why It Is Not Fitted.” It explains that a rigorous degradation model would require multiple laps, variation in TyreLife or stint distance, a comparable pace baseline, and controls for fuel, traffic, weather, and driver effects. It therefore refuses to fit a degradation model and instead treats heavy braking, low-speed apexes, late throttle reapplication, and high speed-loss corners as tyre-load-sensitive indicators only. This is higher-quality scientific reasoning. It avoids producing a sophisticated-looking number that the data cannot actually support.

For this reason, the structured notebook is clearly stronger in model-boundary control. The raw notebook’s tyre stress proxy can be useful as an exploratory metric, but it carries more interpretation risk. The structured notebook’s decision not to model degradation is more professional.

## Presentation and Readability

The raw notebook generates SVG charts using standard-library tools. This has advantages: it is dependency-light, reproducible, and self-contained. It includes a track map, telemetry trace, sector chart, speed-trap chart, and tyre-stress chart. However, the report feels more like an engineering draft. The narrative between tables and charts is shorter, and several important detections depend on thresholds such as the straight-flag and brake-flag definitions. For example, it identifies three speed traps, including one with a peak of only 243.0 km/h, and one trap has a negative speed gain of -8.0 km/h. That does not necessarily make the result wrong, but it suggests the speed-trap windowing may include transitions into braking or lift phases. This reduces the intuitive trustworthiness of the speed-trap summary for a general reader.

The structured notebook uses pandas and matplotlib and reads more like a polished analytical notebook. Its flow is clearer: data loading and validation, derived metrics, sector performance, speed traps, corner and braking analysis, visual report, tyre-modeling boundary, and integrated conclusions. The final insight panel is also more useful as an executive summary. It compresses lap profile, sector balance, straight-line performance, corner performance, driver input profile, and tyre-degradation boundary into concise points.

The structured notebook is not perfect. Its sector calculations rely on sector labels and `segment_dt` from the structured frame, so Sector 1 is reported as 34.105 seconds, while the raw notebook uses official sector timing and reports 33.887 seconds. This is not a large discrepancy, but it shows that the structured version may not always be more precise for official timing boundaries. It also detects only two high-speed traps, while the raw notebook provides three candidate windows. If the research goal is to compare every straight or acceleration zone, the raw notebook may provide broader coverage.

## Final Assessment

If the criterion is final report quality, `pia_jp_26_str.ipynb` is the stronger notebook. Its structure is clearer, its metrics are richer, its corner-level analysis is closer to real motorsport interpretation, its sampling and missing-value discussion is more complete, and it is more careful about not overclaiming tyre degradation from a one-lap dataset. It is the notebook that should be used as the primary report.

If the criterion is transparency from raw data to computed metrics, `pia_jp_26_raw.ipynb` remains important. It demonstrates how to reconstruct an analysis pipeline from unstructured telemetry and lap-summary files, and it validates the basic calculations directly against official timing. It is best used as a method appendix, validation notebook, or audit trail for checking whether the structured data changed any core facts.

The best workflow is to combine both. Keep the raw notebook as the lower-level evidence and validation layer, and use the structured notebook as the main analytical report. The raw notebook can verify lap time, sector sum, distance monotonicity, and speed range. The structured notebook can then provide corner-level interpretation, driving-phase analysis, and the final technical conclusions. This combination preserves traceability while taking full advantage of the improved analytical quality created by structured data.
